# Wild Edible Weeds

The original Mediterranean diet was not arugula from a plastic clamshell. It was dandelions, plantain, mallow, watercress, sorrel, and purslane -- foraged from fields and roadsides, eaten at every meal. These wild plants contain 2-10x the polyphenol and antioxidant content of their cultivated counterparts.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

An ethnobotanical review (Pardo-de-Santayana et al., 2007, PMID 17404895) documented over 200 wild plant species in traditional Mediterranean diets. Morales et al. (2014, PMID 25260557) measured significantly higher antioxidant capacity, total phenolics, and flavonoids in wild edibles compared to commercial cultivated vegetables -- dandelion greens had 3x the polyphenols of cultivated lettuce and more beta-carotene than carrots.

Wild plants produce more polyphenols as defense chemicals against pests, UV, and drought. When we eat them, those same stress compounds activate our own protective pathways. Over 700 species of wild food plants have been documented in traditional Italian cuisine alone (Guarrera & Savo, 2013, PMID 23727631).

## How to Use It

Saute with garlic and olive oil -- the archetype of cucina povera. Eat tender greens raw in mixed salads. Dress bitter greens (dandelion, chicory) with lemon and vinegar to tame the bite. Pair with white beans in the Tuscan tradition. Forage from unsprayed areas only and learn positive identification before harvesting.

## What to Pair It With

| Ingredient | Why | Tradition |
|---|---|---|
| Extra-virgin olive oil | Fat enhances absorption of fat-soluble phytochemicals | Mediterranean |
| Lemon juice | Tames bitterness; enhances iron absorption | Mediterranean |
| Garlic | Sauteed wild greens with garlic is the Mediterranean archetype | Mediterranean |
| White beans | Traditional Tuscan staple with foraged greens | Italian |
| Vinegar | Acidic dressing for bitter greens; traditional preservation | Mediterranean |
| Anchovies | Umami balances bitterness | Southern Italian |

## Flavor Profile

Bitter (dandelion, chicory), peppery (watercress), sour (sorrel, purslane). More complex and assertive than cultivated greens. Often more fibrous. Pungent and mineral-rich -- the taste of the actual landscape.

## The Science

Morales et al. (2014, PMID 25260557) provides the direct comparison between wild and cultivated nutrient density. Pardo-de-Santayana et al. (2007, PMID 17404895) is the definitive ethnobotanical survey of Mediterranean wild food use. Guarrera & Savo (2013, PMID 23727631) documented the Italian tradition extensively.
