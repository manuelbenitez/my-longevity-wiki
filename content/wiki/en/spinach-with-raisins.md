# Spinach With Raisins

Used in multiple Longevity Diet dishes including spinach with raisins and Brussels sprouts — a traditional Mediterranean combination that optimizes iron bioavailability through a clever food pairing.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Spinach is one of the better plant sources of non-heme iron (~2.7 mg/100g), but plant iron has poor absorption without enhancers. Raisins provide fructose and organic acids that act as iron absorption enhancers, similar to ascorbic acid. Hurrell et al. established that organic acids (citric, malic, tartaric) significantly enhance non-heme iron absorption — raisins contain all three plus fructose, which forms soluble iron complexes. The combination is not just traditional but mechanistically sound.

The spinach also provides folate, lutein, zeaxanthin, vitamin K1, and one of the highest dietary concentrations of inorganic nitrate among common vegetables (250–1000 mg NO₃⁻/100g fresh weight). Raisins contribute polyphenols (resveratrol, quercetin derivatives) and a concentrated source of natural sugars for palatability. Together, this dish delivers micronutrient density at low caloric cost.

Olive oil in this preparation dramatically increases lutein and zeaxanthin bioavailability from spinach — both are fat-soluble carotenoids protective against age-related macular degeneration.

### Dietary Nitrate and Nitric Oxide

Spinach's nitrate content activates the enterosalivary nitrate–nitrite–nitric oxide (NO) pathway: oral bacteria on the tongue reduce dietary nitrate (NO₃⁻) to nitrite (NO₂⁻), which is then converted to NO in the acidic stomach environment. NO is a potent vasodilator with antiplatelet and anti-inflammatory actions that improve endothelial function. In a randomized crossover trial of 30 healthy adults, a single spinach-based meal raised plasma NO status from 33 to 86 nmol/L (RXNO), increased plasma nitrite to 99 nmol/L, and produced significant improvements in flow-mediated dilatation and reductions in systolic blood pressure compared to control (p < 0.05) ([Bondonno et al., 2012, *Free Radic Biol Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22019438/)). A meta-analysis of 19 randomized controlled trials subsequently confirmed that inorganic nitrate lowers systolic blood pressure by a mean of −2.42 mmHg (95% CI −4.28 to −0.57; p = 0.01) in healthy individuals ([Zhang et al., 2023, *Food Funct*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36740972/)).

### Lutein, Zeaxanthin, and Brain Health

Spinach is among the richest food sources of lutein (~6 mg/100g cooked), a carotenoid that accumulates selectively in macular tissue and brain gray matter. Fat-soluble carotenoid absorption from spinach is substantially enhanced when eaten with olive oil. Beyond eye health, lutein plasma levels correlate with cognitive performance across multiple domains. A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that dietary lutein supplementation maintained cognitive performance in memory and executive function domains, with change-from-baseline data suggesting a protective effect against decline ([Li & Abdel-Aal, 2021, *Molecules*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34641336/)). Ribaya-Mercado & Blumberg established the mechanistic basis: lutein and zeaxanthin function as antioxidants in the macula and neural tissue, filtering high-energy blue light and quenching reactive oxygen species ([Ribaya-Mercado & Blumberg, 2004, *J Am Coll Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15637215/)).

### Folate and DNA Integrity

At approximately 194 mcg folate per 100g raw, spinach is one of the top food folate sources. Folate functions as a methyl donor in one-carbon metabolism, supplying the methyl groups required for DNA methylation, DNA synthesis, and homocysteine remethylation to methionine. Folate inadequacy raises plasma homocysteine, a marker of cardiovascular and cognitive risk, and impairs the maintenance of genomic DNA methylation patterns. The public health importance of dietary folate from leafy greens like spinach is underscored by a meta-analysis of 24 cohort studies (6,165,894 individuals; 37,280 colorectal cancer cases) finding that high folate intake was associated with a combined relative risk of 0.88 (95% CI 0.83–0.92, p < 0.0001) for colorectal cancer — with the protective effect concentrated at the colon (RR 0.86; 95% CI 0.81–0.92) rather than the rectum ([Fu et al., 2023, *Eur J Cancer Prev*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35579178/)).

### Iron Bioavailability and the Oxalate Question

A persistent concern about spinach iron is its oxalate content, which can bind divalent cations. However, a human stable-isotope study comparing kale and spinach meals found that adding potassium oxalate to kale at levels matching spinach's oxalic acid content (1.26 g) did not significantly alter iron absorption (10.7% vs 11.5%, P = 0.86), suggesting that oxalic acid has minor relevance for iron nutrition from whole foods ([Storcksdieck et al., 2008, *Eur J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17440529/)). Brief cooking further reduces soluble oxalate by 30–87% through leaching. The raisin pairing remains the more functionally important variable: fructose and organic acids in raisins form soluble iron complexes that enhance non-heme iron absorption from the same meal.

## How to Use It

Pairs well with pine nuts, garlic, olive oil. Sauté spinach briefly in olive oil with garlic, then add raisins at the end. Pine nuts add fat and texture. Traditional Spanish and Catalan versions add capers. Serve warm.

## Synergies

- **Olive Oil** (synergy): Fat in olive oil dramatically increases lutein and zeaxanthin bioavailability from spinach.
- **Garlic** (complement): Allicin from garlic and iron from spinach support immune function; garlic enhances spinach's folate retention.
- **Pine Nuts** (complement): Pine nuts provide fat for carotenoid absorption and add pinoleic acid, complementing spinach's micronutrient density.

## Flavor Profile

Taste: savory-sweet, earthy from spinach, sweet from raisins. Aroma: fresh greens, slightly sweet. Texture: wilted tender greens, chewy raisins, crunchy pine nuts. Category: Mediterranean vegetable side dish.

## The Science

- [Hurrell & Egli, 2010, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20200263/): Iron absorption enhancers including organic acids and fructose — present in raisins — significantly improve non-heme iron bioavailability from plant foods like spinach.
- [Ribaya-Mercado & Blumberg, 2004, *J Am Coll Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15637215/): Lutein and zeaxanthin from spinach protect against age-related macular degeneration; fat co-ingestion is essential for absorption of these fat-soluble carotenoids.
- [Bondonno et al., 2012, *Free Radic Biol Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22019438/): RCT (n=30): a single spinach-containing meal raised plasma NO status to 86 nmol/L and improved flow-mediated dilatation and systolic blood pressure vs. control (p < 0.05); nitrate→NO pathway mechanism confirmed.
- [Zhang et al., 2023, *Food Funct*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36740972/): Meta-analysis of 19 RCTs: dietary inorganic nitrate lowers systolic blood pressure by −2.42 mmHg (95% CI −4.28 to −0.57; p = 0.01) in healthy individuals via NO-mediated vasodilation.
- [Li & Abdel-Aal, 2021, *Molecules*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34641336/): Meta-analysis of RCTs: dietary lutein maintained cognitive performance in memory and executive function; spinach provides ~6 mg lutein per 100g cooked, substantially bioavailable with fat.
- [Fu et al., 2023, *Eur J Cancer Prev*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35579178/): Meta-analysis of 24 cohort studies (6.2M participants): high folate intake associated with RR 0.88 (95% CI 0.83–0.92) for colorectal cancer; spinach folate (~194 mcg/100g) represents one of the highest vegetable folate sources.
- [Storcksdieck et al., 2008, *Eur J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17440529/): Stable-isotope human study: added oxalate at spinach-equivalent levels did not significantly alter iron absorption (P = 0.86); raisin organic acids remain the operative iron bioavailability variable.
