# Halibut

A lean white fish providing omega-3 fatty acids (0.35–0.88 g EPA/DHA per 75 g cooked), folate, and magnesium — with a caveat: halibut accumulates moderate levels of methylmercury and the FDA/EPA recommend limiting consumption, particularly for pregnant women and young children.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Halibut occupies an intermediate position among longevity-relevant fish. It provides meaningful omega-3 EPA and DHA, high-quality lean protein, folate for DNA methylation, and magnesium as a cofactor for hundreds of enzymatic reactions. However, as a large flatfish it bioaccumulates mercury more than small oily fish (sardines, anchovies), limiting the frequency of recommended consumption.

Fish consumption is associated with dose-dependent reductions in all-cause mortality and cardiovascular mortality across 58 countries in prospective cohort data, establishing the broad cardiovascular case for including fish in longevity diets ([Mohan et al., 2021, *JAMA Intern Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33683310/)).

Marine omega-3 supplementation at 1 g/day in the VITAL trial significantly reduced major cardiovascular events — confirming EPA and DHA as causally protective rather than merely correlated with better health behaviors ([Manson et al., 2019, *N Engl J Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415637/)).

## How to Use It

Limit to 1–2 servings per week due to moderate mercury content. Prefer lower-mercury alternatives (sardines, anchovies, salmon) when possible. Bake or grill with olive oil, lemon, and capers. Halibut is a good alternative for those who find oily fish too intense in flavor.

## Flavor Profile

Mild, sweet, delicate white flesh. Aroma is clean and oceanic. Texture is firm and meaty with large flakes. Milder than oily fish with no pronounced fishiness. Takes on seasonings readily. Dense enough to grill without falling apart.

## The Science

- [Mohan et al., 2021, *JAMA Intern Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33683310/): Fish consumption inversely associated with all-cause mortality and cardiovascular mortality across 58 countries in prospective cohort analysis.
- [Manson et al., 2019, *N Engl J Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30415637/): VITAL trial found marine omega-3 (1 g/day) significantly reduced major cardiovascular events, confirming causal cardiovascular benefit of EPA and DHA.
