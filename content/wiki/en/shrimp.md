# Shrimp

Shrimp is used in the Longevity Diet's black rice with zucchini and shrimp dish, providing 0.24 g EPA/DHA per 75 g cooked, with lean protein, high selenium, and low mercury.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Shrimp is a lean, low-mercury crustacean with an unusually good nutritional profile per calorie — high in complete protein, selenium, B12, and iodine, with a meaningful marine omega-3 content.

Its cardiovascular profile is nuanced. Childs et al. (1990) found that shrimp consumption raised LDL, but simultaneously raised HDL and reduced triglycerides more than a low-fat diet — the net cardiovascular effect is favorable compared to high saturated fat foods. Dietary cholesterol from seafood matters less for most people than once thought.

Shrimp is also one of the richer food sources of astaxanthin, a carotenoid with 100x greater antioxidant capacity than vitamin E. Astaxanthin's fat-soluble antioxidant properties complement selenium-dependent enzymatic antioxidant systems, both relevant to longevity through oxidative stress reduction.

## How to Use It

Pairs well with black rice, zucchini, garlic. Cook shrimp quickly — 1–2 minutes per side over high heat until pink and just opaque. Overcooking makes them tough. Works well in stir-fries, pasta, rice dishes, and salads.

## Synergies

- **Black Rice** (complement): Black rice anthocyanins and shrimp astaxanthin provide complementary antioxidant protection — a Longevity Diet meal combination.
- **Garlic** (synergy): Garlic allicin and shrimp selenium together activate antioxidant pathways more effectively than either alone.
- **Lemon Juice** (synergy): Vitamin C from lemon improves absorption of shrimp's non-heme iron component.

## Flavor Profile

Taste: sweet, briny, mild umami. Aroma: oceanic, subtle sweetness when cooked, neutral raw. Texture: firm, slightly springy, juicy when not overcooked. Category: shellfish / crustacean.

## The Science

- [Childs et al., 1990, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2349916/): Shellfish consumption raised LDL but simultaneously raised HDL and reduced triglycerides more than a low-fat diet in normolipidemic men — the net cardiovascular lipid effect is favorable.
- [Ambati et al., 2014, *Mar Drugs*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24402174/): Astaxanthin from crustaceans has 100x greater antioxidant capacity than vitamin E and shows anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective, and neuroprotective effects in experimental models.
- Examine.com: Shrimp is high in iodine, selenium, and vitamin B12 with a very lean protein profile (~24 g/100g cooked); low in saturated fat and mercury, making it an optimal longevity protein source.
