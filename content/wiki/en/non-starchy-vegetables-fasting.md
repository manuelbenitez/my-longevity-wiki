# Non-Starchy Vegetables (Fasting)

Non-starchy vegetables are the primary food group permitted during fasting-mimicking diet (FMD) protocols. They provide fiber, polyphenols, and micronutrients with minimal caloric and protein burden, allowing fasting-like cellular responses while maintaining micronutrient adequacy.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

During the FMD protocol, non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, tomatoes, cucumber, zucchini, fennel) serve as the primary food source. Their high fiber and water content support satiety while their minimal protein and calorie content allows the cellular stress responses -- autophagy upregulation, IGF-1 reduction, stem cell regeneration -- that define the fasting-mimicking benefit.

Caloric restriction and related interventions including fasting have extended healthy lifespan across multiple organisms from yeast to mammals; the mechanistic pathways include reduced IGF-1/mTOR/TOR signaling, increased autophagy, and improved cellular maintenance -- all pathways activated when protein and caloric intake is reduced to the levels achievable with a non-starchy vegetable-based FMD ([Fontana et al., 2010, *Science*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20395504/)).

Intermittent fasting and periodic caloric restriction have demonstrated beneficial effects on weight, metabolic health markers, and longevity biomarkers in human clinical trials; non-starchy vegetables as the primary food during fasting windows minimize caloric load while maintaining micronutrient adequacy ([de Cabo and Mattson, 2019, *N Engl J Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31881139/)).

## How to Use It

During FMD days 2–5: build meals around leafy greens (mixed salads, spinach, arugula), cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, kale), tomatoes, cucumber, zucchini, fennel, and celery. Season with olive oil, lemon, and herbs. Avoid starchy vegetables (potatoes, corn, peas) during fasting protocols to minimize glucose and IGF-1 stimulation.

## Flavor Profile

Variable by vegetable. Leafy greens are bitter and fresh; cruciferous are earthy and slightly peppery; tomatoes are sweet-acidic; fennel is sweet-anise. Together they create textural and flavor variety within the severe caloric constraint of fasting days.
