# Sourdough Bread

Slow fermentation degrades 24-70% of the phytic acid that locks up iron and zinc in conventional bread -- turning a staple grain into a significantly more nutritious food.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Grains contain phytic acid, an anti-nutrient that binds iron, zinc, and magnesium, making them largely unabsorbable. Sourdough fermentation solves this. Lactic acid bacteria in the starter produce phytase enzymes that break down phytic acid over 12-24 hours of slow fermentation. A systematic review (Dimidi et al., 2019) found this process degrades 24-70% of phytate, making mineral absorption 2-3x more efficient than in conventional yeast bread.

The benefits extend beyond minerals. A randomized crossover trial (Scazzina et al., 2019) showed sourdough bread produces significantly lower glycemic and insulinemic responses compared to standard baker's yeast bread. The organic acids produced during fermentation slow starch digestion, meaning less glucose spike per slice. Sourdough lactobacilli also partially degrade gluten during fermentation and produce exopolysaccharides that appear to improve gut microbiome composition (Gobbetti et al., 2019).

Fermentation also increases folate content 2-3x through microbial synthesis -- relevant because folate deficiency drives elevated homocysteine, an independent cardiovascular risk factor. The combination of improved mineral bioavailability, lower glycemic impact, and enhanced folate content makes sourdough a meaningfully different food from conventional bread, even when made from the same flour.

## How to Use It

Choose genuine slow-fermented sourdough (not "sourdough-flavored" bread with added vinegar). Look for simple ingredients: flour, water, salt, starter. Whole grain sourdough maximizes fibre and nutrient content. Toast and dip in olive oil to further slow carbohydrate absorption.

## Flavor Profile

Tangy and complex with a lactic sourness that varies by fermentation time and culture. The crust develops nutty, caramelized notes during baking. Texture features a chewy, open crumb with irregular holes and a crisp crust. Longer fermentation produces more pronounced tang. The wheaty, yeasty aroma is distinctive from commercial bread.

## The Science

- Sourdough fermentation reduces phytic acid by 24-70%, improving mineral bioavailability (Dimidi et al., 2019)
- Lower glycemic and insulinemic responses vs. yeast bread in crossover trial (Scazzina et al., 2019)
- Lactobacilli partially degrade gluten and produce beneficial exopolysaccharides (Gobbetti et al., 2019)
- Fermentation increases folate content 2-3x through microbial synthesis
- Iron and zinc absorption 2-3x more efficient than conventional bread
