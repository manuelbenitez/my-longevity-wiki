# Legumes

In every Blue Zone on Earth -- Okinawa, Sardinia, Nicoya, Ikaria, Loma Linda -- researchers found the same dietary pattern: people who live past 100 eat legumes almost every day. A 20g daily increase in bean consumption correlated with a 7-8% drop in mortality.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Legumes are arguably the single most evidence-backed longevity food. Dan Buettner's Blue Zones research (PMID 15172426) identified them as the strongest dietary predictor of survival in elderly populations across five continents. A 2017 meta-analysis in Clinical Nutrition (PMID 28515392) quantified it: each 100g/day increase in legume intake was associated with a 6% reduction in all-cause mortality, with significant protection against coronary heart disease and cardiovascular disease.

The mechanisms stack up. First, fiber: 100g of cooked lentils delivers 7-8g of dietary fiber, a mix of soluble galactooligosaccharides and insoluble cellulose. The soluble fraction feeds Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus in your colon, which ferment it into short-chain fatty acids -- particularly butyrate. Butyrate binds to specific receptors on immune cells to suppress inflammation and increase regulatory T cells. A 2017 study (PMID 28352631) confirmed that legume consumption significantly boosted these beneficial bacterial populations. Meanwhile, the soluble fiber traps bile acids and cholesterol in the small intestine: a 2014 meta-analysis of 26 RCTs (PMID 25061763) found that just one daily serving of pulses lowered LDL cholesterol by 5%.

Second, legumes produce less collateral damage than animal protein. They contain far less choline and L-carnitine -- the substrates gut bacteria convert into TMAO, a molecule linked to a roughly 20% increase in cardiovascular disease risk. And the "second-meal effect" is real: eating chickpeas or lentils at dinner reduces blood glucose not just at that meal, but through the night and into the next morning. The catch -- this effect disappears if legumes are overcooked at high temperatures or if grains are highly milled.

## How to Use It

Cook them gently. Soak dried legumes overnight, drain, and simmer (do not pressure-cook into mush if you want the glycemic benefits). Sprouting or fermenting destroys phytates, which otherwise bind iron, zinc, and calcium. A squeeze of lemon juice at the table can boost non-heme iron absorption 2-6x. Aim for at least one serving (about 75-100g cooked) daily. Canned beans are fine -- just rinse them to cut sodium.

## Flavor Profile

Earthy, nutty, and mildly sweet with a starchy backbone. The aroma is mineral and herbaceous when fresh. Texture ranges from firm and al dente to creamy and smooth when pureed -- think the difference between a French lentil salad and a bowl of hummus. Each variety has its own character: black beans are dense and meaty, red lentils dissolve into silk, chickpeas hold their shape with a satisfying bite.

## The Science

- Blue Zones (Buettner, PMID 15172426): Legumes are the #1 dietary predictor of survival in centenarian populations
- 2017 meta-analysis (PMID 28515392): 6% reduction in all-cause mortality per 100g/day increase
- 2014 CMAJ meta-analysis (PMID 25061763): One daily serving of pulses lowers LDL cholesterol by 5%
- 2017 gut microbiome study (PMID 28352631): Legumes increase Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, and SCFA production
- Examine.com: Strong evidence for glycemic control, LDL reduction, and microbiome diversity
