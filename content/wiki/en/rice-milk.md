# Rice Milk

Rice milk is the Longevity Diet's last resort plant milk — not because it is harmful for most adults, but because it brings almost nothing to the table beyond its allergen profile. It is essentially strained rice water with a fortification packet dissolved in it. If you can tolerate almond, hazelnut, oat, or soy milk, one of those is a better choice. If you cannot tolerate any of them, rice milk is the answer — and it does that job well.

## The Honest Nutritional Picture

Per 100 mL of unsweetened commercial rice milk, the macros are: ~0.3 g protein, ~9–10 g carbohydrate (mostly as free glucose and maltose from enzymatic hydrolysis during production), ~1 g fat, and roughly 47 kcal. Compare that to cow's milk (3.3 g protein, 4.7 g carbohydrate as lactose, 3.5 g fat), almond milk (~0.4 g protein, ~0.3 g carbohydrate, ~1–2 g fat), or soy milk (~3.3 g protein, ~1.5 g carbohydrate).

The glycemic picture is the most clinically significant issue. Rice milk has a glycemic index of approximately 79–100 depending on the product and the reference methodology — among the highest of any plant-based food. A 2023 review of plant-based drink glycemic responses ([Shkembi & Huppertz, 2023, *Foods*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36765982/)) calculated glycemic load values of 18.33 and 16.85 for two commercial rice drinks, categorising them as high-GL beverages, and noted GI estimates "up to 100" — comparable to pure glucose. A 2017 laboratory study of 17 commercial plant-based milk substitutes ([Jeske et al., 2017, *Plant Foods Hum Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27817089/)) measured rice drink GI values of 97.74 ± 6.81 (rice drink natural) and 99.96 ± 5.75 (organic brown rice drink) — values the authors described as comparable to soft drinks and cakes.

The mechanism is straightforward. Commercial rice milk is produced by blending milled rice with water and passing it through amylase enzymes that break down starch chains into shorter dextrins, maltose, and free glucose. By the time the liquid reaches the bottle, the gelatinised starch matrix that slows digestion in whole grain rice has been largely destroyed. The result is a sugar solution that drives a rapid postprandial glucose excursion — the opposite of what the Longevity Diet is trying to achieve with its low-glycemic, anti-inflammatory food pattern.

Resistant starch — the fraction of starch that escapes small-intestinal digestion and reaches the colon as substrate for butyrate-producing bacteria — is minimal in rice milk because the enzymatic processing and liquid form eliminate the physical structures that create resistance. In solid cooked rice, even white rice gains some resistant starch through retrogradation when cooled ([Sonia et al., 2015, *Asia Pac J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26693746/)): fresh cooked white rice contains 0.64 g/100 g resistant starch, rising to 1.65 g/100 g after 24-hour refrigeration and reheating. Rice milk retains none of this; the starch has been enzymatically converted before bottling.

Protein is essentially absent. At 0.3 g per 100 mL — roughly one-eleventh of cow's milk and one-eleventh of soy milk — rice milk cannot contribute meaningfully to daily protein intake. This matters on the Longevity Diet, which already caps animal protein and needs plant protein sources to fill the gap. Using rice milk as a primary beverage while eating a low-protein plant pattern risks inadequate intake without supplementation from legumes, nuts, or other high-protein plant foods.

## The One Clear Win: Hypoallergenic Profile

Rice milk is free of all eight major allergen groups recognised by the US FDA: milk, eggs, peanuts, tree nuts, soy, wheat, fish, and shellfish. It is also gluten-free (provided it is certified, since cross-contamination in milling facilities is possible). This makes it the only widely available plant milk suitable for people with multiple simultaneous food sensitivities — for example, someone with a soy allergy and a tree-nut allergy who also has non-coeliac wheat sensitivity.

For this population the comparison is not "rice milk vs. almond milk." It is "rice milk vs. water in your oatmeal." Framed that way, fortified rice milk is genuinely useful: it delivers calcium (~120 mg/100 mL when fortified with calcium carbonate), vitamin D (~42 IU/100 mL in D3-fortified products), and vitamin B12 in brands that include it — nutrients a multiply-sensitive person might otherwise struggle to obtain from a restricted diet.

Dairy alternatives including plant milks effectively support bone-relevant micronutrient delivery when fortified, and selecting the appropriate type depends on individual nutritional requirements and tolerance constraints ([Ramsing et al., 2023, *Curr Environ Health Rep*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37300651/)). Rice milk's role in that framework is narrow but real.

## The Arsenic Problem

Rice is a semi-aquatic cereal that accumulates inorganic arsenic from paddy soil and irrigation water at rates 10–100× higher than other grains. Inorganic arsenic — as distinct from the less-toxic organic forms — is a Group 1 human carcinogen (IARC classification), with established links to bladder, lung, and skin cancers at prolonged high exposures. This contamination carries over into rice-based products, including rice milk.

The signal in rice milk specifically is well-documented. Meharg et al. ([Meharg et al., 2008, *J Environ Monit*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18385862/)) tested 19 commercial rice milk samples from UK supermarkets and found that 80% had inorganic arsenic concentrations above 10 μg/L — the EU and US maximum contaminant level for drinking water. Some samples reached up to 3× the regulatory threshold (roughly 30 μg/L). Almond- and quinoa-based milks in comparative analyses show inorganic arsenic near or below detection limits.

The practical implication differs by population. For a healthy adult using rice milk occasionally — a splash in coffee, occasional use in baked goods — the arsenic exposure from a glass or two per day is unlikely to approach levels associated with chronic disease risk; the relevant thresholds from drinking water studies involve sustained exposure over years at higher concentrations. But for infants and young children, whose developing neurology is particularly sensitive to arsenic and who may consume rice milk as a primary beverage, the UK Food Standards Agency explicitly advises against rice milk for children under 4.5 years. The recommendation is not precautionary hand-waving: it is based on a calculated body-weight-adjusted exposure that, at infant consumption volumes, approaches or exceeds the daily inorganic arsenic intakes considered problematic.

For adults on the Longevity Diet using rice milk as one of several beverages in a varied diet, the arsenic concern is real but manageable — it argues for moderation rather than avoidance, and for choosing brands that source rice from lower-arsenic growing regions (California-grown US rice tends to have lower arsenic than rice from some South Asian and Gulf Coast US regions, though labelling rarely specifies this).

## How Rice Milk Compares to Other Longevity Diet Plant Milks

The Longevity Diet includes several plant milks as dairy alternatives, and they are not interchangeable.

**Almond milk** is the default. It delivers oleic acid, vitamin E, and phytosterols from the almond base, has a glycemic index around 25–30 (low), and is the milk with the most direct longevity-related evidence behind it when paired with regular whole almond consumption.

**Hazelnut milk** offers a similar story: oleic acid, vitamin E, polyphenols, a low-to-moderate GI, and evidence from hazelnut trials on LDL, flow-mediated dilation, and antioxidant gene expression. Nutritionally superior to rice milk on every dimension except allergen profile.

**Oat milk** is a middle case. Its GI (~69) is well above almond or hazelnut milk, though substantially below rice milk. It contributes beta-glucan fiber — the soluble fiber with demonstrated LDL-lowering effects — but it does contain avenin (oat's prolamin protein), which is problematic for a subset of coeliac patients, and its protein content is higher than rice or almond (~1 g/100 mL).

**Soy milk** is nutritionally the closest to cow's milk — 3.3 g protein per 100 mL, complete amino acid profile, GI around 30–45 — and is the right choice if the primary goal is protein-equivalent dairy substitution and soy is tolerated. Its phytoestrogen content is the reason some people avoid it, though the clinical evidence does not support harm at normal consumption levels in most adults.

**Rice milk**, then, slots in as follows: use it only when the others are excluded by allergy or intolerance. When you do use it, be aware of the high GI and compensate elsewhere in the meal — pairing rice milk with high-fiber foods (oats, chia seeds, legumes) will partially blunt the glycemic excursion through slowed gastric emptying and viscous fiber effects. Do not treat it as a protein source; account for that gap elsewhere.

A broad nutritional assessment of plant-based beverages confirms that fortified versions can match dairy milk for key micronutrients (calcium, vitamin D), but that protein content and overall nutritional profiles vary significantly by type, with rice-based beverages among the weakest on both protein and glycemic metrics ([Smith et al., 2022, *Front Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36003838/)).

## Fortification Guidance

Rice milk's nutrient density depends almost entirely on what the manufacturer adds. The plain liquid — rice, water, enzymes — contains almost no calcium, negligible vitamin D, and no vitamin B12. A well-fortified rice milk label should show:

- **Calcium:** 120–300 mg per 240 mL (from calcium carbonate; bioavailability ~30%, similar to dairy when the carbonate form is used)
- **Vitamin D3:** at least 80–120 IU per 240 mL (D3 preferred over D2 for superior conversion to 25-hydroxyvitamin D)
- **Vitamin B12:** 1–2 μg per 240 mL (relevant for people eating fully plant-based)
- **No added sugar:** check that the carbohydrate listed is from the rice starch hydrolysis, not added cane sugar or rice syrup; sweetened versions add an additional 8–12 g of sugar per 240 mL on top of the already-high intrinsic carbohydrate load

Some brands add vitamin A and riboflavin (B2). These are bonuses, not markers of quality. The three non-negotiables are calcium, vitamin D3, and (for plant-based eaters) B12. Avoid products where the calcium is listed only as "calcium phosphate tricalcium" without a specific mg amount — it signals manufacturers prioritising label optics over actual fortification levels.

Shake before pouring. Calcium carbonate settles rapidly in rice milk due to the low-viscosity base and the absence of stabilising proteins or fats.

## How to Use It

Replace dairy milk 1:1 in hot cereal, baked goods, and coffee. The neutral, mildly sweet flavor disappears into recipes, which is an asset. It froths poorly — the near-total absence of protein and fat means it cannot form a stable foam — so it is not suitable as a barista milk for cappuccinos or lattes unless a specialty barista version with added oil is used.

To partially offset the high GI: pair every serving with a source of soluble fiber or protein. A 240 mL glass poured over steel-cut oats, a tablespoon of ground flaxseed stirred in, or a small handful of nuts alongside will meaningfully slow gastric emptying and flatten the glucose curve relative to drinking it alone or with refined starch.

Do not use as a primary beverage for children under 5 — the arsenic concern and near-zero protein content make it inappropriate as a milk replacement for growing children. Rice milk is an adult food, used in specific circumstances.
