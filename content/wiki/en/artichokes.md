# Artichokes

Among the richest plant sources of prebiotic inulin -- and artichoke leaf extract is one of the few food-derived compounds with Cochrane-level evidence for LDL cholesterol reduction.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Artichokes appear in the Longevity Diet in a Mediterranean spelt salad and as a pizza topping, recommended at 50--80g preserved or canned per serving. They earn their place through two independent mechanisms.

The first is cholesterol. Artichoke leaf extract contains cynarin and luteolin, compounds that inhibit cholesterol synthesis in the liver and stimulate bile acid secretion -- effectively increasing cholesterol clearance. A Cochrane systematic review ([Wider et al., 2009, *Cochrane Database Syst Rev*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19821306/)) concluded that artichoke leaf extract significantly reduced total cholesterol in adults with elevated levels, with a favourable safety profile.

The second mechanism is gut microbiome support. Artichoke is among the richest plant sources of inulin-type fructooligosaccharides (3--10g per 100g fresh). Inulin is a prebiotic fibre that passes through the small intestine undigested and is selectively fermented by Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus species. An in vitro study ([Van den Abbeele et al., 2020, *Nutrients*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32466615/)) confirmed that artichoke extract significantly promoted Bifidobacterium growth while increasing production of short-chain fatty acids -- the metabolites that feed the colon lining, reduce inflammation, and are consistently associated with longevity-associated microbiome profiles.

## How to Use It

Preserved or canned artichoke hearts are nutritionally similar to fresh and far more convenient. Drain and rinse before use. Dress with olive oil, lemon, and herbs for an antipasto. Add to grain salads (spelt, farro) or thin-crust pizza. Artichoke hearts roasted with garlic and olive oil are also excellent as a side or bruschetta topping.

## Flavor Profile

Earthy, mildly bitter, nutty, and slightly sweet. The bitterness comes from cynarin and chlorogenic acid. Cooked hearts have a meaty, tender texture. Preserved in water or oil, the flavour mellows considerably.

## The Science

- [Wider et al., 2009, *Cochrane Database Syst Rev*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19821306/): Cochrane review -- artichoke leaf extract significantly reduced total cholesterol in adults with elevated cholesterol; favourable safety profile.
- [Van den Abbeele et al., 2020, *Nutrients*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32466615/): In vitro study -- artichoke extract significantly increased Bifidobacterium and short-chain fatty acid production in colon microbiome simulation.
- Inulin: 3--10g per 100g fresh artichoke; selectively fermented by beneficial gut bacteria.
