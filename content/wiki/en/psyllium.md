# Psyllium

Psyllium is the only fibre supplement with FDA-approved health claims for both cholesterol lowering and blood glucose reduction. That regulatory bar is high, and psyllium cleared it because the evidence is unusually consistent: it works, it works reliably, and the mechanism is well understood.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

The longevity case for psyllium rests on three pillars: cholesterol, blood sugar, and gut health.

For cholesterol, psyllium's soluble fibre forms a viscous gel in the small intestine that physically traps bile acids, preventing their reabsorption. Your liver then pulls LDL cholesterol from the blood to make replacement bile acids. A controlled trial confirmed that psyllium-enriched cereal significantly lowered total and LDL cholesterol in participants with hypercholesterolaemia ([Olson et al., 1997, *J Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9311953/)), and the book places psyllium within a broader "cholesterol-lowering food portfolio" that achieves combined LDL reductions rivalling statin therapy for moderate-risk individuals.

The quantitative picture from larger meta-analyses is now clear. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 28 RCTs involving 1,924 participants ([Jovanovski et al., 2018, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30239559/)) found that psyllium supplementation at a median dose of 10.2 g/day reduced LDL cholesterol by 0.33 mmol/L (95% CI: −0.38 to −0.27; p < 0.00001), non-HDL cholesterol by 0.39 mmol/L, and apolipoprotein B by 0.05 g/L. The apoB finding is graded as high-quality evidence -- apoB is the most direct measure of atherogenic particle burden, making this a meaningful cardiovascular endpoint beyond LDL alone. The effect is viscosity-dependent: the same fiber in a non-gelled form loses cholesterol-lowering activity, confirming that physical trapping of bile acids, not any chemical interaction, drives the mechanism.

For blood sugar, the same viscous gel slows glucose absorption after meals, flattening the postprandial spike. McRorie (2015, PMID 25972618) calls psyllium the gold standard for gel-forming fibre and notes that no other supplemental fibre has sufficient evidence for this dual health claim.

The glycemic benefit scales with baseline metabolic dysfunction. A meta-analysis of 35 RCTs ([Gibb et al., 2015, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26561625/)) found that psyllium taken before meals produced no significant effect in healthy, euglycemic individuals, modest improvement in those at risk for type 2 diabetes, and the largest response in patients actively treated for type 2 diabetes: fasting blood glucose fell by 37.0 mg/dL and HbA1c fell by 0.97 percentage points (p < 0.001 and p = 0.048 respectively). The dose-response pattern in which benefit scales with glycemic dysregulation suggests that the mechanism -- slowed intestinal glucose absorption via viscous gel -- delivers the most measurable relief when the absorptive system is already overloaded.

### Blood Pressure: A Third Independent Effect

Psyllium's cardiovascular benefits are not limited to cholesterol and glucose. A meta-analysis of 14 RCTs involving 802 participants ([Gholami & Paknahad, 2024, *Food Sci Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39479650/)) found that psyllium supplementation significantly reduced systolic blood pressure by 2.24 mmHg (95% CI: −3.13 to −1.35), with dose and duration showing a dose-response relationship. The proposed mechanism links psyllium-enhanced insulin sensitivity to increased nitric oxide production in vascular endothelium, promoting vasodilation. A 2 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure is modest in isolation but clinically meaningful at the population level, where small persistent shifts in blood pressure distribution translate to large reductions in stroke and coronary event rates.

For gut health, the story gets personal. The book cites evidence that psyllium reduces the severity of colitis and improves gastrointestinal symptoms in ulcerative colitis patients in remission. Jalanka et al. (2019, PMID 30669509) showed that psyllium supplementation increased Faecalibacterium abundance — a butyrate-producing genus that maintains the gut barrier and suppresses inflammation. Butyrate is the primary fuel for colonocytes, and its depletion is a hallmark of inflammatory bowel disease.

### Why Psyllium Is Different From Other Fiber Supplements

Most soluble fibers -- inulin, FOS, partially hydrolyzed guar gum -- are rapidly fermented in the colon and do not form a stable viscous gel in the small intestine. Psyllium's arabinoxylan-based husk is minimally fermented and maintains gel viscosity through the full length of the small intestine, which is precisely where bile acid reabsorption and glucose absorption occur. This structural distinction explains why psyllium has FDA-approved health claims that other fiber supplements have not been able to replicate despite similar fiber content. Dose matters: the FDA health claim for cholesterol reduction specifies 7 g/day of psyllium husk, and studies consistently show greater effects at 10 g/day and above.

## How to Use It

Take 5–10 g of psyllium husk daily, mixed into at least 250 ml of water or another liquid. This is non-negotiable: without adequate water, psyllium can cause intestinal obstruction. Start with a lower dose and increase gradually to avoid bloating. Mix into smoothies, stir into oat porridge, or blend with yogurt. Timing matters less than consistency — take it at whatever point in your day you will actually remember. Some people prefer it 30 minutes before meals to blunt glucose spikes; others add it to breakfast.

## Flavor Profile

Psyllium is essentially flavourless — slightly earthy, slightly hay-like, and entirely unremarkable. This is its greatest culinary advantage: it disappears into almost anything. The texture when hydrated is mucilaginous, which some people find off-putting in plain water but barely notice in a smoothie or porridge.

## The Science

- [Olson et al., 1997, *J Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9311953/): Psyllium-enriched cereals significantly lowered total and LDL cholesterol in controlled trials, providing early RCT evidence for FDA-approved cholesterol health claim.
- [Jovanovski et al., 2018, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30239559/): Meta-analysis of 28 RCTs (n = 1,924) — psyllium at ~10 g/day reduced LDL by 0.33 mmol/L, non-HDL by 0.39 mmol/L, and apoB by 0.05 g/L (high-quality evidence).
- [McRorie, 2015, *Adv Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25972618/): Psyllium is the only fibre with FDA-approved claims for both cholesterol and blood glucose; reviewed mechanisms of viscous gel-forming fiber.
- [Gibb et al., 2015, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26561625/): Meta-analysis of 35 RCTs — psyllium before meals reduced fasting glucose by 37 mg/dL and HbA1c by 0.97% in type 2 diabetes patients, with effect scaling proportionally to glycemic dysregulation.
- [Gholami & Paknahad, 2024, *Food Sci Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39479650/): Meta-analysis of 14 RCTs (n = 802) — psyllium reduced systolic blood pressure by 2.24 mmHg (95% CI: −3.13 to −1.35) with dose-response relationship.
- [Jalanka et al., 2019, *Int J Mol Sci*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30669509/): Psyllium supplementation increased Faecalibacterium (butyrate-producing, gut-barrier-protecting) abundance in healthy adults.
