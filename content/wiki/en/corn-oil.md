# Corn Oil

Listed in the Longevity Diet as a source of vitamin E (1.9 mg per 1 tablespoon, 10% DV). Recommended serving: 1 tablespoon. Corn oil is primarily an omega-6 linoleic acid oil; it reduces LDL cholesterol when substituted for saturated fat, but its high omega-6:omega-3 ratio means it should not be the primary cooking fat.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Corn oil's principal fatty acid is linoleic acid (omega-6 PUFA, ~53g per 100g), with very little omega-3. Replacing dietary saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat including corn oil's linoleic acid does reduce LDL cholesterol and cardiovascular risk -- the American Heart Association's dietary fat advisory ([Sacks et al., 2017, *Circulation*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28620111/)) confirmed this with strong evidence across multiple dietary patterns and populations.

However, the Minnesota Coronary Experiment recovery data, reanalysed by Ramsden et al. ([2016, *BMJ*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27071971/)), complicated the picture: substituting saturated fat with corn oil linoleic acid reduced cholesterol as predicted, but did not significantly reduce cardiovascular events or mortality in the trial, raising questions about the sufficiency of LDL reduction alone when omega-3:omega-6 ratio is skewed.

A systematic review and network meta-analysis of dietary oils ([Schwingshackl et al., 2018, *J Lipid Res*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30006369/)) found that olive oil and canola oil had more favourable effects on LDL, HDL, total cholesterol, and triglycerides compared to corn oil and most other vegetable oils -- explaining the Longevity Diet's preference for extra-virgin olive oil and canola oil as primary fats, with corn oil noted only as a vitamin E source.

Corn oil is also notably rich in phytosterols (~952 mg per 100g), which competitively inhibit cholesterol absorption in the intestine and can reduce LDL by 5--15% at sufficient doses -- an underappreciated benefit.

## How to Use It

Use sparingly -- one tablespoon provides 10% DV vitamin E. Do not use as a primary cooking fat; reserve for occasional use in baked goods or for high-heat cooking when olive oil would smoke. Pair with omega-3-rich foods (fatty fish, flaxseed) to balance the high omega-6 load. Not recommended as a daily staple fat.

## Flavor Profile

Mild, neutral, and faintly sweet. Nearly odourless when refined. Light-bodied with a high smoke point (~232°C), making it suitable for high-temperature cooking. Does not impart distinctive flavour to dishes.

## The Science

- [Sacks et al., 2017, *Circulation*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28620111/): AHA presidential advisory -- replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat (including corn oil linoleic acid) reduces cardiovascular disease risk; strong evidence from RCTs and cohort studies.
- [Ramsden et al., 2016, *BMJ*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27071971/): Re-analysis of Minnesota Coronary Experiment -- corn oil reduced cholesterol but did not reduce cardiovascular mortality, raising questions about omega-6 replacement without adequate omega-3; contextualises why olive oil is preferred.
- [Schwingshackl et al., 2018, *J Lipid Res*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30006369/): Network meta-analysis of 54 RCTs -- olive oil and canola oil have more favourable effects on blood lipid profile compared to corn oil and most other vegetable oils.
