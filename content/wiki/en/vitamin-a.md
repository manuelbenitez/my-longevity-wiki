# Vitamin A

50–90% of US adults are deficient in vitamin A; important for immune function and vision

## Why It Matters for Longevity

50–90% of US adults are deficient in vitamin A; important for immune function and vision Essential for immune cell development, epithelial integrity, and vision (component of rhodopsin in retinal cells).  Alzheimer's disease patients have lower levels of vitamin A; deficiency may contribute to Alzheimer's disease Vitamin A supports neurogenesis and has neuroprotective antioxidant effects; deficiency may accelerate brain aging.  Vitamin A (retinoic acid) activates RAR/RXR nuclear receptors that regulate genes involved in immune function, cell differentiation, and apoptosis — longevity-relevant pathways.

## How to Use It

Pairs well with olive oil, salmon, sweet potato. Use as a nutrient in your daily meals according to the Longevity Diet guidelines.

## Synergies

- **Vitamin E** (synergy): Both fat-soluble vitamins commonly deficient in US adults per the book; taken together in a multivitamin maximizes fat-soluble vitamin coverage  - **Vitamin D** (synergy): Vitamin A and D share nuclear receptor partners (RXR); balanced intake of both optimizes immune regulation and gene expression  - **Olive Oil** (synergy): Dietary fat is essential for absorption of fat-soluble vitamin A; olive oil is the Longevity Diet's primary fat vehicle

## The Science

- [Pino-Lagos et al., 2010, *Biofactors*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20803520/): Retinoic acid is a key regulator of immune cell differentiation and function, acting through RAR/RXR nuclear receptors
- [Ono & Yamada, 2012, *Geriatr Gerontol Int*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22221326/): Reviews evidence that vitamin A plays a neuroprotective role and that deficiency may contribute to Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis
- [Jiménez-Jiménez et al., 1999, *Eur J Neurol*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10362906/): Serum levels of beta-carotene and vitamin A were significantly lower in Alzheimer's disease patients compared to controls
- Examine.com: Preformed retinol (animal sources) is immediately bioavailable; beta-carotene from plants converts at variable rates (1–28%); combining both sources is recommended for adequate status
- Book claim (high confidence): 50–90% of US adults are deficient in vitamin A; important for immune function and vision
- Book claim (medium confidence): Alzheimer's disease patients have lower levels of vitamin A; deficiency may contribute to Alzheimer's disease
