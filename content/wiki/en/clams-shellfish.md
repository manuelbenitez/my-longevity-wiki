# Clams / Shellfish

No food on earth matches clams for vitamin B12 -- 100g of cooked clams delivers over 4,000% of your daily requirement, in a package with less than 150 kcal.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Vitamin B12 deficiency is quietly common, particularly in older adults (10--30% of those over 60 have impaired absorption) and anyone reducing animal foods. Deficiency drives elevated homocysteine -- an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, and osteoporotic fractures. Clams solve this problem more efficiently than any supplement.

Animal-sourced B12 from bivalve mollusks has the highest bioavailability of any dietary source ([Watanabe et al., 2007, *Exp Biol Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17959839/)). Shellfish are also a concentrated source of heme iron (absorbed 2--3x more efficiently than plant non-heme iron) and selenium, a cofactor for glutathione peroxidase, one of the body's primary antioxidant defence enzymes. A comprehensive nutritional review ([Venugopal et al., 2017, *Compr Rev Food Sci Food Saf*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33371588/)) confirmed bivalve mollusks as among the most nutrient-dense animal foods, providing B12, heme iron, zinc, selenium, and omega-3 EPA/DHA with low mercury burden.

Regular fish and shellfish consumption is associated with substantially lower cardiovascular and all-cause mortality. Mozaffarian and Rimm ([2006, *JAMA*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17047219/)) found that 1--2 servings per week of seafood reduced coronary heart disease mortality by ~36% and total mortality by ~17%, with shellfish contributing comparably to oily fish through their combined omega-3, B12, and mineral content.

## The B12 Mechanism in Detail

B12 functions as a cofactor in two enzymatic reactions: methionine synthase (remethylating homocysteine to methionine) and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (converting methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA for entry into the citric acid cycle). Inadequate B12 impairs both, causing homocysteine to accumulate and odd-chain fatty acids to build up in myelin — explaining why B12 deficiency presents both as cardiovascular risk and as neurological deterioration.

A meta-analysis of 21 randomized controlled trials with 1,625 participants found that B12 supplementation significantly lowered plasma homocysteine by a pooled mean of 4.15 μmol/L (95% CI: −4.86 to −3.45; p < 0.001), with greater reductions at doses above 500 μg/day and durations of 12 weeks or longer ([Sohouli et al., 2024, *Nutr Rev*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37495210/)). Two 75 g servings of cooked clams per week would deliver roughly 150–200 mcg of highly bioavailable B12 — well above what is needed to maintain the B12 status required to suppress homocysteine in most adults. The advantage of food-sourced B12 over supplements is that the active transport mechanism in the gut (intrinsic factor) is not saturated by the relatively small bolus delivered per meal, whereas high-dose oral supplements overwhelm this pathway and rely on passive diffusion at substantially lower efficiency.

## Iron: The Density Argument

Clams contain approximately 28 mg of heme iron per 100 g cooked — 155% of the daily value — making them the most iron-dense commonly available food by weight. Heme iron (the form found in animal tissue) is absorbed at 15–35% efficiency compared to 2–20% for non-heme iron from plant sources, and its absorption is not significantly inhibited by phytates, tannins, or calcium — the dietary factors that block non-heme iron uptake. Despite representing only 10–15% of total dietary iron intake in mixed diets, heme iron accounts for over 40% of total iron absorbed because of this absorption advantage.

A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials comparing heme iron directly to non-heme iron interventions found that in children with iron-deficiency anemia, heme iron supplementation produced a clinically significant hemoglobin improvement of 1.06 g/dL (95% CI: 0.34–1.78 g/dL) over non-heme iron. Participants receiving heme iron also experienced 38% fewer gastrointestinal side effects (RR 0.62, 95% CI: 0.40–0.96) — the primary reason iron tablet therapy fails in practice ([Gallo Ruelas et al., 2024, *Eur J Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39708071/)). Food-sourced heme iron from clams delivers these absorption benefits at doses the gut handles in the same way as heme iron from meat, making regular clam consumption a practical iron repletion strategy for individuals who tolerate shellfish.

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency globally, affecting roughly 25% of the world's population. The relevance to longevity is indirect but important: iron deficiency impairs mitochondrial function, reduces oxygen delivery to tissues, promotes fatigue and cognitive impairment, and is associated with worse cardiovascular outcomes. Restoring iron status normalizes these downstream effects without the compliance problems of supplementation.

## Zinc and Immune Function

Cooked clams contain approximately 2–3 mg of zinc per 100 g, though oysters are the shellfish with the highest zinc concentration (over 70 mg per 100 g). Among bivalves, zinc bioavailability is high because the zinc is bound in forms readily released during digestion. Zinc is required for over 300 enzymatic reactions and is essential for T-cell function, wound healing, protein synthesis, and the activity of antioxidant enzymes including superoxide dismutase. Zinc status declines with age, and marginal deficiency is common in older adults consuming low amounts of animal foods.

## Selenium and the Antioxidant Defence System

Clams provide approximately 64 mcg of selenium per 100 g cooked (116% DV). Selenium is incorporated into selenoproteins — notably glutathione peroxidase (GPx) and thioredoxin reductase — that neutralize reactive oxygen species, protect cell membranes from lipid peroxidation, and regenerate vitamins C and E from their oxidized forms. Clams deliver selenium in organic form (primarily selenomethionine and selenocysteine), which is more bioavailable than inorganic selenium from supplements.

The cardiovascular relevance of adequate selenium status extends beyond antioxidant activity. A meta-analysis of 43 randomized controlled trials found that antioxidant supplements containing selenium were associated with a 23% reduction in cardiovascular mortality (RR 0.77, 95% CI: 0.62–0.97), whereas antioxidant mixtures without selenium were not protective — and some were associated with increased all-cause mortality (RR 1.09) ([Jenkins et al., 2020, *Am J Clin Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33053149/)). This finding positions selenium not as a passive antioxidant component but as a required cofactor for the other antioxidants in the network to produce net benefit. Dietary selenium from clams supports this protective system continuously, in the organic forms that the body can distribute to selenoprotein synthesis rather than relying on absorption of inorganic selenite or selenate.

## Taurine and Cardiovascular Function

Clams, mussels, and oysters are among the richest dietary sources of taurine, a sulfur-containing amino acid present in concentrations of 240–520 mg per 100 g of cooked bivalve tissue — substantially higher than beef, chicken, or dairy. Taurine is not strictly essential (humans can synthesize it from cysteine and methionine) but synthesis capacity is limited, and tissue levels decline with age, making dietary sources increasingly important in older adults.

Taurine's cardiovascular effects operate through at least three mechanisms: modulation of intracellular calcium handling in cardiomyocytes (reducing arrhythmia susceptibility), osmoregulation in vascular endothelial cells, and inhibition of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system components that drive hypertension. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 20 randomized controlled trials in 808 participants found that taurine supplementation significantly reduced systolic blood pressure by 4.0 mmHg (95% CI: −7.29 to −0.71 mmHg), diastolic blood pressure by 1.44 mmHg (95% CI: −2.48 to −0.39 mmHg), and resting heart rate by 3.58 bpm (95% CI: −6.04 to −1.11 bpm). Left ventricular ejection fraction increased by 4.98 percentage points (95% CI: 1.56–8.41) and NYHA functional class improved by 0.40 points (95% CI: −0.52 to −0.28) in heart failure patients ([Tzang et al., 2024, *Nutr J*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39148075/)). The simultaneous effects on blood pressure, heart rate, and cardiac function make taurine one of the more mechanistically complete cardioprotective nutrients in the shellfish package.

## Mercury Profile

Unlike larger marine predators, clams and other bivalves are filter feeders that occupy a low position in the food chain. Their methylmercury content is consistently negligible — typically 0.002–0.009 ppm — more than 100-fold lower than tuna. This means the full nutritional payload (B12, heme iron, zinc, selenium, omega-3) is delivered without the mercury offset that complicates the risk-benefit calculation for large predatory fish.

## How to Use It

Steam clams in white wine and garlic to preserve nutrients. Canned clams retain most B12 and are a practical option. Two servings of shellfish per week maintains robust B12 status. Mussels and oysters offer similar benefits if clams are unavailable.

## Flavor Profile

Briny and sweet with pronounced umami depth and mineral undertone. Aroma is clean and oceanic with a faint iodine note. Texture ranges from tender to pleasantly chewy depending on species and cooking time. Overcooking turns them rubbery -- pull them the moment shells open.
