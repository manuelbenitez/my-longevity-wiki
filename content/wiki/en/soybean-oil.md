# Soybean Oil

Listed as a source of omega-3 ALA (0.31 g per 1 tsp) and vitamin E (1.1 mg per 1 tbsp, 6% DV) in the Longevity Diet — used sparingly, not as the primary cooking fat.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Soybean oil is the world's most consumed vegetable oil, and its omega-6 to omega-3 ratio (~7:1) is notably better than corn oil (~50:1) but substantially worse than olive oil (~9:1 in favor of MUFA). The Longevity Diet recommends it in moderation as a secondary oil, not as a replacement for extra-virgin olive oil.

The relationship between linoleic acid (soybean oil's dominant fatty acid at 51%) and inflammation is genuinely contested. Johnson & Fritsche's 2012 systematic review of 15 clinical trials found no consistent evidence that dietary linoleic acid increases inflammatory biomarkers at realistic intake levels — contradicting the popular narrative that all omega-6 is pro-inflammatory.

However, soybean oil's high polyunsaturated fat content makes it vulnerable to oxidative degradation at high cooking temperatures, generating potentially harmful lipid peroxidation products. Olive oil's predominantly monounsaturated fatty acid composition is more thermostable, which is why it remains the Longevity Diet's primary fat for cooking.

## How to Use It

Use in cold preparations or low-heat cooking when olive oil flavor is undesirable. Not recommended as a primary cooking oil. Canola oil or walnut oil provide better omega-6:omega-3 ratios for occasional olive oil substitution.

## Synergies

- **Olive Oil** (antagonism): Olive oil is the preferred cooking fat in Longevity Diet; soybean oil's high omega-6 content makes it a less favorable alternative.
- **Omega-3 Fatty Acids** (complement): ALA in soybean oil contributes modestly to omega-3 status; best used sparingly alongside marine omega-3 sources.
- **Walnut Oil** (complement): Both are plant-based omega-3 containing oils; walnut oil has a more favorable omega-6:omega-3 ratio.

## Flavor Profile

Taste: neutral, very mild. Aroma: neutral, faintly beany. Texture: liquid, light. Category: cooking oil.

## The Science

- [Johnson & Fritsche, 2012, *J Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22889633/): Systematic review of 15 RCTs: dietary linoleic acid at realistic intakes did not increase arachidonic acid or inflammatory biomarkers — the pro-inflammatory narrative for omega-6 is not supported by controlled human trials.
- [Simopoulos, 2016, *Nutrients*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26950145/): An elevated omega-6/omega-3 ratio (as seen in Western diets dominated by soybean oil) increases risk for obesity and chronic disease; the ancestral ratio was ~1:1.
