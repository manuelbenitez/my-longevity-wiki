# Vitamin E

50–90% of US adults are deficient in vitamin E; important for essential body functions. Deficiency has been implicated in brain aging and dementias; Alzheimer's disease patients have lower serum vitamin E levels.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Vitamin E (alpha-tocopherol) is the primary fat-soluble antioxidant in cell membranes: it protects polyunsaturated fatty acids from lipid peroxidation, regulates platelet aggregation, and modulates NF-κB inflammatory signaling.

Morris et al.'s (2002) JAMA study of 815 community-dwelling residents found that dietary vitamin E intake — but not vitamin C or beta-carotene — was associated with a significantly reduced risk of incident Alzheimer's disease over 3.9 years of follow-up. This was a dietary intake finding: food sources showed the protective association, reinforcing the Longevity Diet approach of meeting nutrient needs primarily through food.

The critical caveat: high-dose vitamin E supplements (400–800 IU/day alpha-tocopherol) have produced mixed or harmful results in trials, including a slight increase in all-cause mortality in meta-analyses. This argues for obtaining vitamin E through food — wheat germ oil, almonds, hazelnuts, sunflower seeds, olive oil — rather than high-dose supplementation.

## How to Use It

Pairs well with almonds, olive oil, sunflower seeds. Use as a nutrient in your daily meals according to the Longevity Diet guidelines. 1 tablespoon of wheat germ oil delivers the full RDA (15 mg); 1 oz almonds provides ~7.3 mg.

## Synergies

- **Vitamin A** (synergy): Both fat-soluble vitamins commonly deficient in US adults per the book; co-supplementation in a multivitamin every 2–3 days is recommended.
- **Vitamin C** (synergy): Vitamin C regenerates oxidized vitamin E, restoring its antioxidant capacity; together they form the primary lipid and aqueous antioxidant defense system.
- **Olive Oil** (synergy): Olive oil is a primary dietary source of vitamin E; its oleic acid also spares vitamin E from oxidation, enhancing effective status.

## The Science

- [Morris et al., 2002, *JAMA*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12076219/): Dietary vitamin E — but not vitamin C or beta-carotene — was inversely associated with incident Alzheimer's disease in a prospective community study (n=815, 3.9-year follow-up).
- [Miller et al., 2005, *Ann Intern Med*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15537682/): Meta-analysis of 19 clinical trials: high-dose vitamin E supplementation (≥400 IU/day) was associated with increased all-cause mortality, supporting food-first approach.
