# Sole

Sole is a lean flatfish providing complete protein, modest EPA/DHA (0.5 g/100g cooked), and very low mercury — a safe weekly rotation fish in the Longevity Diet.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Sole contributes to the Longevity Diet's core principle of 2–3 weekly fish servings: it's low in mercury, lean, and provides complete protein with all essential amino acids. Its EPA/DHA content is moderate rather than high, so it works best paired with oily fish (sardines, trout) earlier in the week to hit weekly omega-3 targets.

Fish consumption broadly reduces cardiovascular and all-cause mortality even from lean species. Jayedi et al.'s 2018 dose-response meta-analysis of 14 prospective cohort studies (911,348 participants) found cardiovascular mortality declined with increasing fish consumption regardless of species type.

Sole's phosphorus content (248 mg/100g) is notable — it contributes to bone mineral matrix alongside calcium, and fish phosphorus is highly bioavailable compared to plant sources.

## How to Use It

Pairs well with lemon, capers, olive oil. Classic preparations: sole meunière (butter-browned with lemon), piccata (capers and lemon), or simply baked with olive oil and herbs. Very thin fillets cook in 2–3 minutes per side — overcooking makes them fall apart.

## Synergies

- **Sardines** (complement): Sole is low in omega-3; pair with sardines or anchovies elsewhere in the week to hit EPA/DHA targets.
- **Capers** (complement): Classic piccata preparation; capers add antioxidant quercetin and flavonoid content to a lean fish meal.
- **Lemon** (complement): Classic European pairing; vitamin C adds to mineral absorption and brightness to delicate flavor.

## Flavor Profile

Taste: very mild, delicate, sweet. Aroma: neutral, fresh oceanic. Texture: very delicate, tender, flaky. Category: white flatfish.

## The Science

- [Jayedi et al., 2018, *Crit Rev Food Sci Nutr*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29317009/): Dose-response meta-analysis: increasing fish consumption inversely associated with cardiovascular and all-cause mortality across 14 prospective cohort studies; benefits consistent across fish types.
- [Mozaffarian & Rimm, 2006, *JAMA*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17047219/): Fish consumption cardiovascular benefits substantially exceed mercury risks for low-mercury species; sole's mercury content (~0.05 ppm) is among the lowest of any fish.
