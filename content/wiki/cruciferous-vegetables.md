# Cruciferous Vegetables

A compound in broccoli reactivates one of your body's most powerful tumor suppressors -- a gene called PTEN that literally stops cancer cells from growing -- and it was published in *Science*, not a wellness blog.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

Cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, kale, Brussels sprouts, cabbage, bok choy) contain glucosinolates that break down into some of the most studied anti-cancer compounds in nutrition science. The three heavyweights are sulforaphane, indole-3-carbinol (I3C), and their downstream metabolites.

Sulforaphane activates the Nrf2 pathway, your cells' master switch for phase 2 detoxification enzymes. These enzymes neutralize carcinogens before they can damage DNA. A meta-analysis of 62 studies found that higher cruciferous vegetable intake was inversely associated with cancers of the stomach, colon, rectum, and lung, with relative risk reductions of 16-22%. A separate prospective meta-analysis linked higher intake to 22% lower total mortality and significantly reduced cardiovascular disease mortality.

Indole-3-carbinol does something different and remarkable. It inhibits an enzyme called WWP1, which normally keeps the tumor suppressor PTEN in check. Block WWP1 and PTEN is free to do its job -- suppress tumor growth. This was demonstrated in cancer-prone mice and published in *Science* in 2019. Mice with extra PTEN activity live longer, full stop.

Beyond cancer, sulforaphane has shown metabolic benefits. An RCT using broccoli sprout extract showed improved fasting glucose and reduced HbA1c in type 2 diabetes patients, working through the same Nrf2-mediated pathway.

## How to Use It

Here's the catch: sulforaphane doesn't exist in the plant. Its precursor, glucoraphanin, needs an enzyme called myrosinase to convert it. Chopping or chewing raw cruciferous vegetables activates myrosinase. But cooking destroys the enzyme.

The workaround is elegant: add a pinch of mustard seed or mustard powder to cooked cruciferous vegetables. Mustard contains its own myrosinase, which restores the conversion. Alternatively, chop your broccoli and let it sit for 40 minutes before cooking -- this gives myrosinase time to do its work before heat deactivates it.

Broccoli sprouts deserve special mention. They contain 10-100x more glucoraphanin than mature broccoli heads. A small handful on a sandwich or salad delivers a concentrated dose.

Steam rather than boil. Steaming preserves both vitamin C and more of the glucosinolate content. Aim for 3-5 servings of cruciferous vegetables per week.

## Flavor Profile

Raw cruciferous vegetables are peppery, bitter, and sulfurous with a satisfying crunch. Cooking tames the bitterness and brings out sweetness -- especially roasting, which caramelizes the natural sugars. Brussels sprouts get sweeter after frost. The sulfurous aroma is strongest when boiled (another reason to steam or roast instead). These vegetables are workhorses across Mediterranean, East Asian, Indian, and Northern European cuisines.

## The Science

- Liu et al. (2013): Meta-analysis of 62 studies; cruciferous vegetable intake inversely associated with stomach, colon, rectal, and lung cancer risk (RR 0.78-0.84). (PMID: 23679348)
- Wu et al. (2015): Prospective meta-analysis; higher cruciferous intake linked to 22% lower total mortality (HR 0.78). (PMID: 25644351)
- Yu et al. (2019): Indole-3-carbinol inactivates WWP1, reactivating PTEN tumor suppressor. (*Science*, DOI: 10.1126/science.aaw5154)
- Singh et al. (2014): Broccoli sprout extract improved fasting glucose and HbA1c in type 2 diabetes RCT. (PMID: 24481154)
- Examine.com: Sulforaphane activates Nrf2-dependent antioxidant response; strongest cancer evidence for bladder and lung.
